# Minimum Evolvable Product (MEP) - Learnings

**Date**: 2026-01-19  
**Phase**: P1-P2 (Product Strategy)  
**Source**: YouTube Video - "How To Get Your First Users" by YC General Partner Ankit Gupta (0kARDVL2nZg)

---

## What Worked

- **MEP Concept**: Understanding that MVP isn't enough - we need a Minimum Evolvable Product that can adapt
- **Search vs Persuasion**: Framing early user acquisition as a search problem, not persuasion
- **Early Adopter Mindset**: Recognizing that early adopters exist and have specific characteristics
- **Path Dependency**: Understanding that early users shape product evolution (Tesla case study)

## What Didn't

- **Traditional MVP Thinking**: Just building features without considering adaptability
- **Free User Strategy**: Not charging early means missing out on sharper feedback from paying customers
- **Mass Market Focus**: Trying to appeal to everyone instead of finding early adopters first

## Key Insights

### 1. Minimum Evolvable Product (MEP) > MVP

**Insight**: MVP focuses on viability, but MEP focuses on adaptability. The product should be simple but able to respond to market pressures and evolve.

**Application to RYLA**:
- Build core features that can adapt based on user feedback
- Don't over-engineer - build simple, evolvable foundation
- Focus on "surviving contact" with early users, not perfection

**Action Items**:
- [ ] Review current MVP scope - ensure it's evolvable, not just viable
- [ ] Design features with adaptation in mind
- [ ] Build feedback loops into product from day one

### 2. Finding Early Users is a Search Problem

**Insight**: It's not about persuading people - it's about finding the right people (the "Gustafs" and "Ankits") who:
- Love being early adopters
- Have burning needs your product can solve
- Are willing to try new products

**Application to RYLA**:
- Target AI influencer creators who are actively looking for tools
- Find people frustrated with current solutions
- Use targeted personal outreach, not mass marketing

**Action Items**:
- [ ] Identify early adopter personas (AI influencer creators, content creators)
- [ ] Create targeted outreach strategy
- [ ] Build "wide surface area" for early users to find us

### 3. Charge Real Money Early

**Insight**: Early adopters aren't price sensitive. Paying customers give sharper feedback than free users. The goal isn't revenue—it's feedback.

**Application to RYLA**:
- Don't wait to charge - even small amounts create commitment
- Paying customers will give better feedback
- Price sensitivity comes later, not from early adopters

**Action Items**:
- [ ] Review EP-003 (Payment Funnel) - ensure we can charge early
- [ ] Set pricing that captures value but isn't barrier for early adopters
- [ ] Use payment as signal of real interest

### 4. Product Evolution is Path-Dependent

**Insight**: Early users don't just give feedback—they steer how your product evolves. Tesla's early adopters wanted tech and acceleration, which shaped their entire product line.

**Application to RYLA**:
- Early users will determine which features matter most
- We may discover use cases we didn't anticipate
- Product direction will be shaped by who uses it first

**Action Items**:
- [ ] Study early users closely (anthropologist approach)
- [ ] Track what features early users actually use
- [ ] Be ready to pivot based on early user behavior

### 5. Experiment Fast, Don't Fear Churn

**Insight**: Run constant experiments on pricing, landing pages, onboarding, features. Don't stress about losing users—there are plenty more who haven't heard of you yet.

**Application to RYLA**:
- Test different pricing models
- Experiment with onboarding flows
- Try different feature combinations
- Don't be afraid to lose users who aren't a good fit

**Action Items**:
- [ ] Set up A/B testing infrastructure
- [ ] Create experiment backlog
- [ ] Track churn but don't overreact

### 6. Consumer vs Prosumer/Business Decision

**Insight**: Consumer apps struggle with AI costs. Many AI founders start by selling to prosumers or businesses. Personal software spend is tiny (~$150/month), while corporate cards have multiple tools costing more.

**Application to RYLA**:
- May need to consider prosumer/business model
- Consumer pricing may not cover AI costs
- Early users may reveal which model works better

**Action Items**:
- [ ] Monitor AI costs vs consumer pricing
- [ ] Consider prosumer tier (power users, agencies)
- [ ] Evaluate business model based on early user feedback
