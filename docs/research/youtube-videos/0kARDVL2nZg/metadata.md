## Summary

This video by YC General Partner Ankit Gupta presents an update to the classic MVP playbook: the **Minimum Evolvable Product (MEP)**. The core thesis is that when starting out, you don't just need a minimum viable product—you need a product that can adapt and evolve based on feedback from early users.

Key insights:
- Finding first users is a **search problem, not a persuasion problem**
- Early adopters exist (the "Gustafs" and "Ankits" of the world) who love trying new products or have burning needs
- Product evolution is **path-dependent** on what early adopters want
- Early users don't just give feedback—they **steer how your product evolves**
- The Tesla Roadster case study shows how early adopters shaped Tesla's entire product line

## Key Points

1. **Minimum Evolvable Product (MEP) vs MVP**
   - MVP focuses on viability
   - MEP focuses on adaptability and evolution
   - Product should be simple but able to respond to market pressures

2. **Finding Early Adopters**
   - It's a search problem, not persuasion
   - Look for people who love being early adopters (like Gustaf from Airbnb)
   - Find people with burning needs who will try anything
   - Use targeted personal outreach, not billboards

3. **Counterintuitive Rules**
   - **Charge real money early** - Early adopters aren't price sensitive, paying customers give sharper feedback
   - **Launch early** - Engineer wide surface area for early users to find you
   - **Study early users closely** - Be like an anthropologist discovering a hidden civilization
   - **Experiment fast, don't fear churn** - Run constant experiments on pricing, landing pages, onboarding, features

4. **Path Dependency**
   - Product evolution depends on what early adopters wanted
   - Tesla example: Early adopters cared about tech and acceleration, not comfort
   - This shaped Tesla's entire product line (Model Y has faster 0-60 than Lamborghini but worse suspension than Toyota)

5. **The Algorithm**
   - Start with an "amoeba" (basic functions)
   - Run evolutionary search through potential future directions
   - Early users push product toward certain features
   - Product morphs from amoeba to mature organism
